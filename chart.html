bilabial
This is a consonant that you use both lips to pronounce. Try the "m" sound in "man" or "p" in "pat".
Bilabial consonants on Wikipedia
labiodental
This is a consonant that you use your lower lip and upper teeth to pronounce. Think the "f" or "v" sound in English.
Labiodental consonants on Wikipedia
dental
This is a consonant that is pronounced by putting your tongue against (under) your upper teeth. Try the "th" sound in "think".
Dental consonants on Wikipedia
alveolar
This is a consonant pronounced by taking the tip of your tongue and putting it on the roof of your mouth right behind your front teeth. Try the "n" sound in "run" or "l" in "luck".

It's called this because the alveolar ridge is that area behind your front teeth on the roof of your mouth.
Alveolar consonants on Wikipedia
post alveolar
This is a consonant pronounced with your tongue touching (or almost touching) the roof of your mouth, but behind where it is for an alveolar consonant. Think "sh" in English.
Post alveolar consonants on Wikipedia
retroflex
Wikipedia says this type of consonant requires a flat, concave, or curled tongue, and your tongue is somewhere around the middle of the top of your mouth. However, English doesn't have an retroflex consonant sounds (at least, Wikipedia says there aren't) so I'm not really sure what this sound is. Will update as I learn more.
Post alveolar consonants on Wikipedia
palatal
This is a consonant pronounced by placing the body of your tongue near the middle of the roof of your mouth. Try the "y" sound of "yes".
Palatal consonants on Wikipedia
velar
This is a consonant pronounced by moving the back part of your tongue towards the back of the roof of your mouth. Try the "ng" sound in English.
Velar consonants on Wikipedia
uvular
This type of consonant pronounced by moving the back of your tongue towards the uvula, in the very back of your mouth. English doesn't have any sounds like this though.
Uvular consonants on Wikipedia
pharyngeal
A pharyngeal consonant is pronounced but putting the root of the tongue against the pharynx, in the very back of your mouth where your mouth meets your throat/nasal cavity. English doesn't have any pharyngeal consonants.
Pharyngeal consonants on Wikipedia
glottal
Also called laryngeal consonants, these are pronounced using the glottis, the space between the vocal chords. For example, the English "h" (as in "hat") is pronounced by closing the space between the vocal chords.
Glottal consonants on Wikipedia
